Community Health Integrated Platform (CHIP) V2.0

Streamlining Healthcare Operations at the Grassroots.

Project Overview

CHIP V2.0 is a healthcare software developed by Khushi Baby NGO to digitize and manage public health operations at the last mile. The platform includes modules for Login, Attendance (Punch-IN), User Profiles, Digital Health Survey (DHS) Role-Based Access Control (RBAC), and critical health programs like ABHA linkage, Family Planning, and tracking Maternal and Infant Mortality (IMR/MMR).

Project type: End-to-end app + branding

Industry

Public Health

Tools

Duration

Context & Background

CHIP is accessed by healthcare workers across a three-tiered hierarchy:

  • Village Level: ASHA workers
  • Sub-Health Center (SHC) Level: ANMs and CHOs
  • Primary Health Center (PHC) Level: Medical Officers (MOs), Staff Nurses, Pharmacists, and Lab Technicians

To maintain data integrity and workflow clarity, the app is governed by a Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) System, which ensures that each user only accesses relevant modules and permissions based on their designation and facility level.

User Needs & Business Goals

  • Field users needed streamlined workflows tailored to their daily tasks.
  • Supervisors required visibility into data across levels without clutter.
  • Khushi Baby NGO aimed to ensure security, accuracy, and hierarchy alignment across users.

Field Observations (Shadowing ASHAs and ANMs)

To understand the real-world context of CHIP usage, I conducted immersive field visits to rural areas. I shadowed ASHAs and ANMs during their daily routines, particularly while they:

  • Conducted household visits for DHS and other health-related data collection
  • Interacted with community members, often in challenging physical or social conditions
  • Used CHIP on low-end Android phones, sometimes with intermittent network or power access

User Interviews

I conducted semi-structured interviews with healthcare workers and supervisors across all three levels:

  • ASHAs & ANMs: Focused on ease of use, language comprehension, offline needs
  • CHOs & MOs: Focused on monitoring, verification, and dashboard utility
  • Pharmacists & Lab Technicians: Focused on task-specific modules like stock entry and test results

These sessions helped identify pain points, mental models, and expectations from CHIP across different roles.

Key Insight

  • Users preferred default field values and auto-filled inputs where possible.
  • Supervisors wanted real-time syncing and completion alerts
  • Each role required tailored interfaces, not just filtered versions of the same screen.

To maintain data integrity and workflow clarity, the app is governed by a Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) System, which ensures that each user only accesses relevant modules and permissions based on their designation and facility level.

Contextual Inquiry

While users performed tasks like DHS entry or Punch-IN, I asked them to “think aloud” to capture their natural workflow, hesitation points, and expectations.

This was especially helpful in identifying:

  • Confusing terminology
  • Repetitive or unintuitive field orders
  • Overload from long surveys or multi-tab forms

Key Insight

  • Users struggled with long questions long questions and often skipped fields they assumed weren’t applicable.
  • Skip logic was essential to reduce mental load.
  • Supervisors wanted real-time syncing and completion alerts
  • Field-level hints and error validation reduced hesitation during form input.

Design Process

Ideation & Concept Development

After identifying user pain points and contextual challenges from research, I moved into structured ideation to explore meaningful solutions for CHIP. My process blended problem framing, divergent exploration, and convergent refinement to serve diverse user roles—from ASHAs to Medical Officers.

Problem Framing

I broke down the complex user journeys into role-specific problem statements:

  • “How might we simplify long survey forms for ASHAs while minimizing data errors?”
  • “How might CHOs and ANMs track field-level progress without switching tools?”
  • “How might we manage access across roles while ensuring data integrity?”

These statements helped focus each solution direction while accounting for RBAC governance and technical limitations like offline mode.

Design Principles

To guide all design decisions, I established key principles based on field insights:

  • Simplicity over density: Prioritize clarity for low-literate users
  • Offline-first: Ensure critical workflows work without network
  • Modularity: Each module should be usable independently and together
  • RBAC Awareness: Interface adapts dynamically to user designation
  • Guided Progress: Step-by-step flow for tasks like DHS and Family Planning

Concept Exploration

During ideation, I explored multiple UI concepts for each core module of CHIP. Every concept was evaluated based on user role, workflow complexity, and technical feasibility, especially in low-connectivity environments.

This was especially helpful in identifying:

Punch-IN / Punch-OUT

  • Concept 1: Simple tap-to-log system
  • Concept 2 (selected): Geo-tagged Punch-IN with time stamp, visual feedback, and status history

Why this works: Promotes accountability, offers visibility to supervisors

ABHA ID Integration

  • Concept 1: Separate workflow post-registration
  • Concept 2 (selected): Optional ABHA linking during or after registration via banner prompt

Why this works: Keeps flow optional and non-blocking while promoting health stack integration

Usability Testing

To ensure that the CHIP application was intuitive, efficient, and usable across all health worker roles, I conducted multiple rounds of usability testing in both simulated and real-field settings.

Objectives

  • Validate the clarity of workflows for each module (DHS, FP, Punch-IN, etc.)
  • Identify pain points in navigation, language comprehension, and data entry
  • Measure task completion time and error rates
  • Ensure offline functionality behaves as expected in remote settings
  • Gather user sentiment and feedback on visual hierarchy and interactions

These statements helped focus each solution direction while accounting for RBAC governance and technical limitations like offline mode.

Participants

Usability testing was conducted with 8 target users across roles and geographies:

  • 3 ASHAs from rural villages
  • 2 ANMs stationed at SHCs
  • 1 CHO responsible for supervision
  • 1 Medical Officer & 1 Staff Nurse at PHC level

Each user interacted with prototypes on their actual devices (mid-range Android phones or tablets).

Final Solution

Overview of the Final Design

The CHIP application was designed to be role-sensitive, offline-capable, and workflow-driven — addressing real-life needs of ASHAs, ANMs, CHOs, and PHC-level staff across rural healthcare settings.

  • Homepage and navigation auto-adjust based on the user's role and facility level.
  • Punch-IN reflects GPS and timestamp clearly.
  • RBAC implemented via user profile at login and governed across UI components.

Conclusion

Project Reflection
