Community Health Integrated Platform (CHIP) V2.0

Streamlining Healthcare Operations at the Grassroots.

Project Overview

CHIP V2.0 is a healthcare software developed by Khushi Baby NGO to digitize and manage public health operations at the last mile. The platform includes modules for Login, Attendance (Punch-IN), User Profiles, Digital Health Survey (DHS) Role-Based Access Control (RBAC), and critical health programs like ABHA linkage, Family Planning, and tracking Maternal and Infant Mortality (IMR/MMR).

Project type: End-to-end app + branding

Industry

Public Health Digitalisation

Tools

Figma, FigJam, Jira, Confluence & Google Meet

Duration

Q3 2023

Problem Statement

Exploring the complexities of healthcare data management in rural settings, focusing on streamlining workflows for diverse frontline workers, ensuring role-specific access, and overcoming the barriers of low digital literacy and connectivity to improve accuracy, efficiency, and data security.

Context & Background

CHIP is accessed by healthcare workers across a three-tiered hierarchy:

  • Village Level: ASHA workers
  • Sub-Health Center (SHC) Level: ANMs and CHOs
  • Primary Health Center (PHC) Level: Medical Officers (MOs), Staff Nurses, Pharmacists, and Lab Technicians

To maintain data integrity and workflow clarity, the app is governed by a Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) System, which ensures that each user only accesses relevant modules and permissions based on their designation and facility level.

User Needs & Business Goals

  • Field users needed streamlined workflows tailored to their daily tasks.
  • Supervisors required visibility into data across levels without clutter.
  • Khushi Baby NGO aimed to ensure security, accuracy, and hierarchy alignment across users.

Research

Conducting in-depth field observations (shadowing ASHAs and ANMs), user interviews, and contextual inquiry to gain a comprehensive understanding of healthcare workers' workflows, challenges, and needs, ensuring that design solutions are rooted in real-world behavior and aligned with public health program goals.

Field Observations (Shadowing ASHAs and ANMs)

To understand the real-world context of CHIP usage, I conducted immersive field visits to rural areas. I shadowed ASHAs and ANMs during their daily routines, particularly while they:

  • Conducted household visits for DHS and other health-related data collection
  • Interacted with community members, often in challenging physical or social conditions
  • Used CHIP on low-end Android phones, sometimes with intermittent network or power access

Key Insight

  • DHS forms are lengthy and repetitive, which increases fatigue and the risk of errors.
  • Users were more confident with step-by-step flows, where the form is broken into manageable sections.
  • Offline capability and autosave were non-negotiable due to inconsistent connectivity.

These insights were crucial in refining the design to improve efficiency, reduce errors, and better accommodate the unique challenges faced by frontline workers.

User Interviews

I conducted semi-structured interviews with healthcare workers and supervisors across all three levels:

  • ASHAs & ANMs: Focused on ease of use, language comprehension, offline needs
  • CHOs & MOs: Focused on monitoring, verification, and dashboard utility
  • Pharmacists & Lab Technicians: Focused on task-specific modules like stock entry and test results

These interviews provided valuable insights into pain points, mental models, and expectations from CHIP across different roles.

Key Insight

  • Users preferred default field values and auto-filled inputs where possible.
  • Supervisors wanted real-time syncing and completion alerts
  • Each role required tailored interfaces, not just filtered versions of the same screen.

These findings were essential in refining the user experience to better address the distinct needs of each role within the system.

Contextual Inquiry

While users performed tasks like DHS entry or Punch-IN, I asked them to “think aloud” to capture their natural workflow, hesitation points, and expectations.

This was especially helpful in identifying:

  • Confusing terminology
  • Repetitive or unintuitive field orders
  • Overload from long surveys or multi-tab forms

Key Insight

  • Users struggled with long questions long questions and often skipped fields they assumed weren’t applicable.
  • Skip logic was essential to reduce mental load.
  • Supervisors wanted real-time syncing and completion alerts
  • Field-level hints and error validation reduced hesitation during form input.

These insights played a key role in optimizing the form design to improve usability and reduce user friction.

Each persona was crafted to ensure that the design solutions would address the specific pain points and tasks of these diverse roles, from data entry to monitoring and verification.

Design Process

Ideation & Concept Development

After identifying user pain points and contextual challenges from research, I moved into structured ideation to explore meaningful solutions for CHIP. My process blended problem framing, divergent exploration, and convergent refinement to serve diverse user roles—from ASHAs to Medical Officers.

Problem Framing

I broke down the complex user journeys into role-specific problem statements:

  • “How might we simplify long survey forms for ASHAs while minimizing data errors?”
  • “How might CHOs and ANMs track field-level progress without switching tools?”
  • “How might we manage access across roles while ensuring data integrity?”

These statements helped focus each solution direction while accounting for RBAC governance and technical limitations like offline mode.

Design Principles

To guide all design decisions, I established key principles based on field insights:

  • Simplicity over density: Prioritize clarity for low-literate users
  • Offline-first: Ensure critical workflows work without network
  • Modularity: Each module should be usable independently and together
  • RBAC Awareness: Interface adapts dynamically to user designation
  • Guided Progress: Step-by-step flow for tasks like DHS and Family Planning

Concept Exploration

During ideation, I explored multiple UI concepts for each core module of CHIP. Every concept was evaluated based on user role, workflow complexity, and technical feasibilityw, especially in low-connectivity environments.

This was especially helpful in identifying:

Punch-IN / Punch-OUT

  • Concept 1: Simple tap-to-log system
  • Concept 2 (selected): Geo-tagged Punch-IN with time stamp, visual feedback, and status history

Why this works: Promotes accountability, offers visibility to supervisors

ABHA ID Integration

  • Concept 1: Separate workflow post-registration
  • Concept 2 (selected): Optional ABHA linking during or after registration via banner prompt

Why this works: Keeps flow optional and non-blocking while promoting health stack integration

These explorations helped balance usability, accountability, and technical constraints—ensuring that each design decision was role-appropriate, minimally disruptive, and aligned with both user behavior and broader health system integration.

User Flows

Designing Scalable, Role-Based Flows for a Focused and Efficient Experience

  • App navigation is contextualized by role—ASHAs see fewer modules, while MOs have dashboard and supervision features.
  • Created modular flows so features could scale based on access level.

These modular, role-based flows ensured that each user experienced a focused and intuitive journey—minimizing cognitive load for frontline workers while enabling supervisors to seamlessly monitor and manage broader program operations.

Wireframes & Prototyping

Low- to mid-fidelity wireframes were created to visualize key modules—like DHS, ABHA, and Family Planning—ensuring clarity in structure and flow. Interactive prototypes allowed for early validation with users and stakeholders, helping refine navigation, logic, and screen behavior before development.

Visual Design

To design CHIP for real-world usability, I created role-based personas that represent the distinct needs, behaviors, and constraints of users across different healthcare levels. These personas helped ensure that each interface was tailored, contextual, and respectful of users’ daily challenges.

  • Color-coded modules help distinguish workflows per role.
  • Icons + Labels used to assist semi-literate users.
  • Responsive layout adapted to Android phones commonly used in the field.

Iteration & Testing

Usability Testing

To ensure that the CHIP application was intuitive, efficient, and usable across all health worker roles, I conducted multiple rounds of usability testing in both simulated and real-field settings.

Objectives

  • Validate the clarity of workflows for each module (DHS, FP, Punch-IN, etc.)
  • Identify pain points in navigation, language comprehension, and data entry
  • Measure task completion time and error rates
  • Ensure offline functionality behaves as expected in remote settings
  • Gather user sentiment and feedback on visual hierarchy and interactions

These statements helped focus each solution direction while accounting for RBAC governance and technical limitations like offline mode.

Participants

Usability testing was conducted with 8 target users across roles and geographies:

  • 3 ASHAs from rural villages
  • 2 ANMs stationed at SHCs
  • 1 CHO responsible for supervision
  • 1 Medical Officer & 1 Staff Nurse at PHC level

Each user interacted with prototypes on their actual devices (mid-range Android phones or tablets).

Methods

A mix of contextual testing, technical safeguards, and scalable design patterns were used to ensure CHIP works reliably in real-world rural settings.

Method Purpose
Moderated Field Testing Real-time feedback while using the app on-site
Offline Support All critical functions work offline with smart sync handling
Security & Access Fine-grained control through RBAC
Data Quality Real-time validation, skip logic, and progressive save enhance accuracy
Scalability Modules designed to plug-and-play across health programs and states

By grounding our methods in real field conditions and long-term program needs, we ensured CHIP could scale across regions while remaining dependable, secure, and easy to use for every healthcare worker.

Final Solution

Overview of the Final Design

The CHIP application was designed to be role-sensitive, offline-capable, and workflow-driven — addressing real-life needs of ASHAs, ANMs, CHOs, and PHC-level staff across rural healthcare settings.

  • Homepage and navigation auto-adjust based on the user's role and facility level.
  • Punch-IN reflects GPS and timestamp clearly.
  • RBAC implemented via user profile at login and governed across UI components.

Login & Registration (RBAC Enabled)

Secure and Role-Aware Onboarding for Every User

The login and registration module ensures that users access only the features relevant to their role through RBAC. With OTP-based login, QR scanning for facility codes, and a guided first-time setup, the flow is built to be both secure and simple—especially for users with limited digital literacy.

Key Features:

  • OTP-based login with RBAC (Role-Based Access Control)
  • First-time registration flow with facility & geography linking
  • QR-based facility code scannings

Benefits:

  • Ensures secure and role-appropriate access
  • Simplifies onboarding for digitally less-experienced users
  • Reduces setup errors by linking users directly to their facility

Punch-IN / Punch-OUT System

Reliable Attendance Tracking Built for the Field

This module enables healthcare workers to log their attendance with geo-tagged timestamps, even in low-connectivity areas. With offline caching, sync retries, and clear visual feedback, it helps maintain transparency and enables supervisors to monitor field activity in real time.

Key Features:

  • Geo-tagged time logging
  • Offline caching with sync retry
  • Visual feedback with status history

Benefits:

  • Improves accountability and transparency of field visits
  • Enables supervisors to track staff presence in real time
  • Works reliably in low-network zones

Digital Health Survey (DHS)

Streamlined, Error-Resistant Surveying for Frontline Health Workers

The DHS module guides users through complex health data collection with modular forms, skip logic, and autosave functionality. Designed for offline use, it supports various survey types while reducing errors and effort through smart validations and data reuse.

Key Features:

  • Modular guided form with skip logic
  • Autosave and sync queue for offline mode
  • Support for household, maternal, child, and facility sections

Benefits:

  • Standardizes field data collection across health themes
  • Minimizes errors with validation and visual sectioning
  • Saves time by reusing previously captured data

ABHA ID Integration

Digital Health Survey (DHS)

Seamless Health ID Linking Without Disrupting Workflows

This module enables optional ABHA ID creation or linking during or after registration, supporting both Aadhaar and mobile-based methods. With a guided, non-blocking flow, it promotes interoperability with India’s digital health stack—without slowing down frontline tasks.

Key Features:

  • Optional linking during registration or from profile
  • Step-by-step flow with guided tooltips
  • ABHA fetch via Aadhaar or mobile

Benefits:

  • Integrates health records with national health stack
  • Promotes interoperability
  • Flexible enough to avoid friction during time-sensitive workflows

Family Planning (FP) Module

Proactive Tracking and Counseling for Better Family Planning Outcomes

The FP module helps ASHAs select, record, and follow up on contraceptive methods through visual aids and personalized timelines. With built-in reminders and resupply tracking, it supports continuity of care and reduces dropouts through timely, informed engagement.

Key Features:

  • Selection of FP methods with visual options
  • Timeline of FP history per beneficiary
  • Auto-reminders for follow-up and resupply

Benefits:

  • Empowers ASHAs to track continuity in contraceptive use
  • Supports proactive counseling and resupply
  • Reduces dropout through better visibility and reminders

Profile – Facility, Geography & User

Smart Profiles That Adapt to Roles, Locations, and Hierarchies

This module manages accurate role-based user details, including facility and geographic mapping via QR scan. With support for multi-role logins, it streamlines access for rotating staff and ensures clean data for reporting and audits across locations.

Key Features:

  • Editable role-based profile
  • QR scan to map to facility
  • Multi-role login support (for supervisors)

Benefits:

  • Ensures accurate user-facility mapping
  • Simplifies the app for rotating staff
  • Supports audit trails and reporting by geography

Role-Based Homepages

A Personalized Starting Point for Every User Role

The role-based homepage adapts to each user’s responsibilities—showing only relevant tasks, alerts, and shortcuts. By minimizing clutter and prioritizing daily actions, it boosts focus, reduces errors, and encourages consistent, confident app use.

Key Features:

  • Dynamic dashboard per role (ASHA, ANM, CHO, MO, etc.)
  • Pending tasks, alerts, sync status display
  • Smart shortcuts for daily workflows

Benefits:

  • Reduces cognitive overload by showing only what’s needed
  • Helps users focus on high-priority tasks
  • Encourages consistent usage through intuitive layout
